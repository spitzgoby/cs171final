The War on Drugs

In the more than 40 years since President Nixon declared a war on drugs (1), substance abuse remains high, and the number of drug induced deaths has increased year after year for the past decade (2) and the costs of the war continue to grow, and currently stand at $51,000,000,000 per year (3).

The Changing Face of Substance Abuse in America

The abuse of opioid medications, such Oxycontin®, Vicodin®, and Demerol® has dramatically increased in recent years. Unintentional poisoning deaths from prescription opioids has increased 400% from 1999 to 2010 and now outnumber those from heroin and cocaine combined (4). From 2003 to 2013 the number of people seeking treatment for synthetic opioids other than methadone increased 300% (5). People may assume prescription pain relievers are less dangerous than illegal drugs because a doctor prescribes them, but when abused, these drugs can result in addiction, overdose, and death, especially if combined with other drugs or alcohol.

There is concern that abuse of prescription opioids may ultimately lead to heroin use. From a recent study “Nearly half of young people who inject heroin surveyed in recent studies reported abusing prescription opioids before starting to use heroin. Some individuals reported switching to heroin because it is cheaper and easier to obtain than prescription opioids” (6)

As the Economy Sinks, Drug use Rises

As median income falls, drug induced deaths rise. The same pattern appears with employment data, as the number of unemployed people increases, the rate of drug-induced deaths also increased in lock step. Surprisingly theses effects are concentrated among prime-age white males with low educational attainment (9).

Death Rate and Factor Year Slider

It takes some time before economic factors begin to have an effect on death rates. A person may begin using drugs as the result of job loss and not pass away for several years. Conversely an upswing in median income would take several years to manifest in itself in lower death rates. The slider allows you to compare economic factors from one year against death rates from a different year.
