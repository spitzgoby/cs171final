The War on Drugs

In the more than 40 years since President Nixon declared a war on drugs (1) , substance abuse remains high, and the number of drug induced deaths has increased year after year for the past decade (2) and the costs of the war continue to grow, and currently stand at $51,000,000,000 per year (3).

President Richard Nixon, in 1968. (Photo: Ollie Atkins, White House photographer)

The Changing Face of Substance Abuse in America

The abuse of opioid medications , such Oxycontin®, Vicodin®, and Demerol® has dramatically increased in recent years. Unintentional poisoning deaths from prescription opioids has increased 400% from 1999 to 2010 and now outnumber those from heroin and cocaine combined (4). From 2003 to 2013 the number of people seeking treatment for synthetic opioids other than methadone increased 300% (5) . People may assume prescription pain relievers are less dangerous than illegal drugs because a doctor prescribes them, but when abused, these drugs can result in addiction, overdose, and death, especially if combined with other drugs or alcohol.

There is concern that abuse of prescription opioids may ultimately lead to heroin use. From a recent study “Nearly half of young people who inject heroin surveyed in recent studies reported abusing prescription opioids before starting to use heroin. Some individuals reported switching to heroin because it is cheaper and easier to obtain than prescription opioids” (6)

Opioids

Opioids include any substance that acts on opioid receptors in the brain. Opium, an opioid in use since antiquity is the forerunner of morphine and heroin. Morphine rose to prominence in the early 19th century due to its miraculous ability to eliminate sever pain, the addictive qualities of Morphine did not go unnoticed. In the second half of the 19th century, Bayer, a German pharmaceutical company discovered and patented heroin, and declared it a safer less addictive alternative to morphine for the management of sever pain. Pain management remains the most common medical use of opioids. In the modern era we have a greater selection of opioids, including synthetic and semi-synthetics variants such codeine, hydrocodone, oxycodone and fentanyl, but the dangers and challenges of addiction remain. As the Economy Sinks, Drug use Rises

As median income falls, drug induced deaths rise. The same pattern appears with employment data, as the number of unemployed people increases, the rate of drug-induced deaths also increased in lock step. Surprisingly theses effects are concentrated among middle aged white males with little more than a high school education (8).

Seeking Help

From 2003 until 2013, the years for which data is available, there was a pronounced change in the types of substances American’s were seeking treatment for. Treatment for cocaine, and crack cocaine peaked in 2006, with over 270 000 people seeking in treatment, but by 2013 the number seeking treatment had dropped to less than 103 000. In fact every substance tracked is at or near a ten year low for admission to treatment programs with one exception, opioids. In 2013 the number of people seeking treatment for addiction to opioids, which includes substances such as heroin, methadone, and morphine had increased by over 45% since 2004. Those seeking treatment for heroin and methadone has remain relatively constant, and all of the growth has been those seeking treatment for addiction to synthetic opioids, which is composed of prescription pain medication such as Oxycontin®, Vicodin®, and Demerol® (9).

Hope

The rising rates of addiction and death surrounding opioids paint a bleak picture, but there is hope. The federal government is starting to take action. In 2015 the U.S. Health and Human Services (HHS) created targeted initiates intended to reduce overdoses and addiction to prescription opioids and heroin. President Obama’s 2016 budget contains $133 million in net new funding for efforts aimed to reduce the abuse and misuse of opioids. The HHS’s has identified three priority areas that directly confront the current opioid crisis (10).

  • “Providing training and educational resources, including updated prescriber guidelines, to assist health professionals in making informed prescribing decisions and address the over-prescribing of opioids."
  • "Increasing use of naloxone, as well as continuing to support the development and distribution of the life-saving drug, to help reduce the number of deaths associated with prescription opioid and heroin overdose."
  • "Expanding the use of Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT), a comprehensive way to address the needs of individuals that combines the use of medication with counseling and behavioral therapies to treat substance use disorders.”
The War on drugs has been costly in terms of money spent and the number of lives shattered and lost due to substance abuse. Rather than focusing on drug users as people to be persecuted and prosecuted, our laws and policies need to be reformed with a more compassionate approach that seeks to prevent and treat addiction rather than punish it.
